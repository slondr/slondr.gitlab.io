What We Believe Ourselves To Be

  • Science fiction can "arouse strong beliefs," and thus is useful to explore philosophical topics
  • Parfit proposes two sci-fi thought experiments, the first of which is broken into three parts

The Teletransporter

Regular travel:
I travel to Mars via spaceship, for several years
Simple Teletransporter:
My mind & body are obliterated and recreated perfectly atom-by-atom on Mars
Branch-Line Case:
My mind & body are Replicated perfectly atom-by-atom on Mars; I suffer cardiac arrest and die on Earth days later

The Teletransporter (Cont)

  • Some people would view the Simple Teletransporter as merely travel; others would view it as death + creation of a new person
  • The Branch-Line Case seems to intuitively support the 2nd view
    • But is that really true?

Criteria of Personal Identity

  • To answer this question, we need to qualify what personal identity is. There's lots of competing criteria
  • The Billiard Ball Example; The Chrysalis Example; The Wristwatch Example

Division

  • My body is destroyed. At the same time, the minds (brains) of both of my identical twin brothers are destroyed. By a miracle of science, my brain is divided equally and each half is transplanted into the body of one of my brothers. My brain function is not impaired.
  • Who am I?

Division (Cont)

There are four possibilities:

  1. I am dead.
  2. I survive as one of the two people.
  3. I survive as the other of the two people.
  4. I survive as both people.

(1) is clearly false; (2) and (3) are unjustifiably arbitrary, so (4) must be true!

Parfit performs a logical analysis to prove this:

Personal Identity and Relation R

Relation R:
Psychological connectedness and/or continuity, with the right kind of cause.
Personal Identity:
Relation R + uniqueness

For our purposes, "the right kind of cause" can be any cause.
